## From Questioning to Compression

1. The more skilled AI becomes at *giving answers*, the more essential it is for you to master *asking questions*.  
2. For most people, AI is an amplifier of ability—but for a few, it’s first and foremost an amplifier of *ambition*. Most use it to save time; a few use it to host bolder imaginations—like launching projects they’d never dared dream of ten years ago. AI doesn’t amplify skill first—it amplifies whether you’re willing to treat your own life as a larger experiment.  
3. As large models grow more capable, what remains uniquely human—and differentiated—is your values, taste, and insight.  
4. You’re not using AI to create work. You’re using your work to train your *mirror self*. Every output is a self-cloning event.  
5. The better you are at writing prompts, the more deeply you understand *human frailty*: clarity, constraint, compression, prioritization, counterexamples, boundaries…  
6. Future founders won’t need answers—they’ll need to design *questions scalable by AI*.  
7. Information is no longer scarce. What *is* scarce is your personal *compression function*. AI can expand infinitely; you must compress. In the AI era, compression ability is the new IQ.  
8. AI may be the first tool in history that cares more about *who you actually are* than what you can do.  
9. Learning fast is still slower than one structural cognitive reframe. Learning adds incrementally; reframing leaps. When was the last time you changed your *mental framework*—not just your technique?  
10. You’re not teaching AI to become smarter. You’re teaching it to replicate your soul. What AI enhances isn’t just your strengths—it’s your blind spots, too.  
11. The more you rely on AI, the more vital it is to cultivate your *irreplaceable human parameters*: values, taste, judgment, worldview, aesthetics. Right now—what’s the most *human parameter* worth having a model learn from you?

## High Standards  

High standards aren’t just a work habit—they’re your operating system.

One of the deepest impressions I carried from working with a former leader was his foundational professional principle: *If we decide to do something, we do it as well as it can be done—or we don’t do it at all.*

At first, it felt overwhelming.

But after being pushed through discomfort and struggle, I realized: high standards are among the strongest forces for expanding your capability boundary.

They let you grow faster in the same amount of time.  
See deeper layers in the same task.  
Rise more naturally in the same competition.

When everyone defaults to “good enough,”  
we default to “best” as the *minimum acceptable version*.

Even a 1% edge, compounded over a year, becomes undeniable.

You’ll notice: your output, quality, rhythm, and efficiency quietly shift into another dimension—distinct from your peers.  
All because of one simple habit: no superficiality, no shortcuts, no compromises.

What people fear most isn’t hard work—it’s mistaking cleverness for competence, and laziness for efficiency.  
Those little “lucky breaks” and “time-savers”? They always come back.  
They leave you hollow when complexity arrives, hesitant when difficulty looms—because deep down, you know your foundation isn’t strong enough to hold bigger opportunities.

True mastery isn’t about working harder. It’s about making *“the best I’m capable of right now”* your automatic setting.  
Once that sinks in, the world changes: you spot essence faster, build judgment structures more easily, and distinguish effort that yields results from effort that merely burns time.

More importantly, high standards radiate outward—from person to team, from individual to environment.  
You lift the water level around you.  
That environmental compounding effect is incalculable.

So this mindset bears repeating: *Either don’t start—or start with the intent to deliver the best possible version.*  
Effort poured into excellence never vanishes.  
Effort spent on compromise always leaves a trace.

Your real growth speed depends on the standard you apply—to yourself—in each moment.  
And high standards? They’re always the hardest—and most worthwhile—kind of difficulty.

## Principles and Rationality  

Many family conflicts aren’t caused by wrong methods—but by unclear principles.

Set principles clearly, align on them, and uphold them—even in moments of tension—and most friction dissolves naturally.

For example, in our home, we hold three small but firm principles:

- Never speak negatively about the other parent in front of the children.  
- When one parent is guiding or correcting a child, the other does not undermine or contradict.  
- Use “we” instead of “I” as often as possible—so children experience their parents as a unified whole.

Living these out consistently demands real rational discipline.

Fatigue, emotion, resentment, miscommunication—all can make us forget our principles in an instant. A child’s offhand comment. A partner’s tired sigh. And just like that, reason drops away, replaced by reflex.

That’s precisely why principles matter most *in those moments*.  
Principles are the line—and the habit—that decides for you when emotion surges.  
Emotions shift. Principles hold.

Children need that reliability.  
They develop security, trust, and sound judgment not in chaos—but in environments where rules are stable, predictable, and upheld.

When parental responses are random or mood-dependent—“Yes today, no tomorrow”; “Negotiable now, non-negotiable later”; “United this morning, divided by lunch”—children stop attending to logic. They start reading *atmosphere* instead.

That’s not the outcome we want.

**Reasoned dialogue is the hardest—and most vital—skill a family can practice.**  

It’s not coldness. It’s a gentler way of caring—for each other, and for the child.

- It lets us handle facts before feelings in conflict.  
- It teaches children the world is navigated by logic—not guesswork.  
- It keeps the family system intact, even when emotions run high.

The more we practice reason, the clearer the core truths become:  
Real love isn’t choosing the path that feels easiest *right now*.  
It’s choosing the path that serves the family’s long-term health—even when it’s harder.

Over time, families stabilize. Children settle. Adults mature.  
Principles, then, are quiet, tender power—letting us live fully human lives, even amid feeling.

## Wealth-Invisible Living  

On “keeping wealth invisible”:

1. “Wealth-invisible” ≠ being low-key.  
2. “Wealth-invisible” = preventing wealth from distorting others’ mental model of who you are.  
3. Human perception is multi-variable. When “wealth” suddenly dominates that model, other variables—competence, contribution, character—get compressed. That’s *single-variable misjudgment*.  
4. True wealth-invisibility means letting others see your *value structure first*, your *wealth structure second*.  
5. Put differently: When you succeed, the more you make “money” seem unimportant in your story, the more people will see *you*—not just your net worth.  
6. The wealthier you become, the more actively you must craft *narratives unrelated to wealth*.  
7. Wealth itself isn’t the problem. The absence of *anti-wealth narratives* is.  
8. Why doesn’t Elon Musk—despite his vast fortune—feel like he’s “showing off”? Because he’s spent decades building a dominant alternate narrative: adventurer, engineer, relentless disruptor, someone who literally risked his life building rockets.  
9. In that narrative, wealth carries almost zero weight.  
10. Wealth-invisibility isn’t hiding money—it’s ensuring “wealth” is never the *first variable* people use to assess you.  
11. A basic feature of human cognition: we reduce complex individuals to simple labels.  
12. Real masters operate via emotional calibration, scene selection, primary narrative design—and deliberate variable-weight shifting.

## Memento Mori  

I asked Gemini 3 to build a small tool: *Memento Mori — Life Weeks Visualized*. It works beautifully.

Link: [laoyao.cn](https://www.laoyao.cn/ai/life.html)

Enter your birth date and expected lifespan—and the tool renders your life in three visual states: **Past**, **Now**, and **Future**.

As shown here:  

![](/assets/cognitive-notes/images/《姚金刚认知随笔》-image-85.png)

Stare at that page long enough—and a deeper question emerges:  
*Am I living my own story? Or someone else’s expectation? Or a script handed to me?*

*Memento Mori*: Remember, you will die.

Your life isn’t an infinite canvas. It’s an oil painting drying in real time.

The power of this phrase lies not in death—but in the sudden, visceral awareness that your time is finite, bounded, and irrevocable. That’s *mortality awareness*.

Its purpose isn’t fear. It’s clarity. It invites you to ask:

- Which parts of this life truly belong to *me*?  
- Which are just noise from society?  
- Which paths am I walking only because I’ve never questioned them?  
- Which choices did I accept without ever choosing?

Mortality awareness isn’t pessimism—it’s radical lucidity. It gives you permission to:  
Reject meaningless busyness.  
Release stories that aren’t yours.  
Reclaim time from others.  
Return your life—fully—to yourself.

In a finite life, your greatest freedom is deciding *where each brushstroke lands*.

## AI & The Future, Part II  

*The Silicon World*, our live discussion series on “AI and the Future,” held its second session Saturday night—covering eight tightly framed topics:

1. When was the last time an AI-related moment shifted your worldview?  
2. Why do some still reach for traditional tools before AI—even when stuck? Is the gap habit, inertia—or something deeper? When will “everyone uses AI daily” arrive? Is it generational? How far off is “AI-as-smartphone”?  
3. What major, previously unmet needs or large-scale scenarios have recently become viable *because of AI*?  
4. If AI-generated art or writing surpasses humans in both quality and speed by orders of magnitude—what’s left for “average human creators”?  
5. Which futures described in *The Three-Body Problem* are inevitable—and which are fiction?  
6. At 10-, 20-, 50-, and 100-year horizons: what will AI be capable of—and what will it change?  
7. If an ordinary person had just 10 hours to learn AI seriously—how would you allocate those hours across three concrete actions?  
8. Thirty years from now, what one sentence do you hope 20–30-year-olds say about *our generation*?

Gemini 3 distilled the full transcript into *The Silicon World Core Ten*—a concise synthesis of ideas that reshape cognition, action, business judgment, and ultimate values.

**I. Cognitive Reframe (Breaking the Old World)**

### 1. “Experience” Is Becoming a Liability  
> **Core insight (Fuxiang / Chen Xiang):** “The era of eating experience is over.”  
> This is the harshest workplace truth. When an intern + API can replace a decade-old security team, the moat of accumulated “proficiency” collapses. The future belongs to explorers—not veterans.

### 2. Art and Technique Are Now Fully Decoupled  
> **First-principle insight (Niksi):** “Before, making art required mastering technique (e.g., piano). AI decouples them. Now, aesthetic sense alone unlocks creation.”  
> That’s why AI content explodes: “creator” now means *taste* and *imagination*—nothing else.

### 3. Entertainment Is the Real On-Ramp to Mass AI Adoption  
> **Counterintuitive insight (Niksi):** “The day ‘everyone uses AI daily’ arrives—not because people use it for work—but because fun, playful AI products go viral.”  
> History repeats: internet and smartphones spread first through games and social apps. So will AI. Don’t fixate on productivity tools—you’ll miss the biggest entry point.

**II. Action Guide (Surviving the New World)**

### 4. The “50-Keyword” Breakthrough Method  
> **Most effective learning method (Yao Jingang):** “Pick a new domain. Identify its core 50–100 keywords. Ask AI to explain each—then teach *it* back using the Feynman technique.”  
> In an age of knowledge overload, this is the fastest, lowest-cost way for anyone to build a functional mental model from zero.

### 5. Don’t Treat AI as a Tool—Treat It as a Mentor  
> **Key mindset (Yuanzi):** “Don’t just search. Set it as a respected teacher—and prepare thoughtful questions before asking.”  
> Prompting isn’t incantation. It’s posture. The richness of your context—the respect you show—directly determines the density of its intelligence.

### 6. Business Opportunity: Releasing Suppressed Demand  
> **Commercial logic (Chen Xiang):** “Human needs haven’t changed. AI’s opportunity lies in lowering the cost of fulfilling *previously suppressed* needs—like 1:1 tutoring or therapy.”  
> This is the litmus test for spotting real vs. fake demand: don’t invent new wants—remove old barriers.

**III. Human Grounding (Holding the Line)**

### 7. The Essence of Creation: “Because I Exist”  
> **Emotional anchor (Yao Jingang):** “Even if AI paints better, my daughter’s drawing still matters—because this world exists *only because I exist in it*.”  
> When AI outperforms humans in every skill, this subjective, embodied presence remains our sole, unassailable dignity—and our final moat.

**IV. Ultimate Horizon (Looking Farther Ahead)**

### 8. *The Three-Body Problem*—Real-World Mapping  
> **Masterful analogy (Fuxiang):** “Luo Ji (the Swordholder) = Ilya Sutskever (guardian of safety); Wei Cheng (ruthless pragmatist) = Sam Altman (relentless accelerator); Cheng Xin = European regulators; the Dark Forest = Anthropic.”  
> Not just clever wordplay—it’s the sharpest, most accurate portrait of today’s global AI power dynamics. Understand this map, and you understand Silicon Valley.

### 9. The Endgame Roadmap: AGI → Robot → Rocket  
> **Long-view forecast (Fuxiang):** “10 years: AGI (digital world). 20 years: robots (physical world). 50 years: interstellar travel (cosmic world).”  
> This is civilization’s main storyline—a grand coordinate system to orient every step we take today.

### 10. “The Beginning of Prosperity”  
> **Ultimate framing (Fuxiang):** “In 30 years, people will say: *‘That generation—30 years ago—was the beginning of sustainable human prosperity.’*”  
> Amid widespread anxiety, this fiercely optimistic definition is a shot of adrenaline. It reminds us: **We stand at history’s hinge. We are the ancients of a new age—and the architects of abundance.**
