## Living Joyfully and Efficiently  

Staying focused requires having focus *to begin with*.  

Do the most important thing—and only one thing at a time.  

Add small rituals to anchor your day. For example, I now start each workday with a cup of coffee—a simple, intentional cue that signals: *Let’s begin.*  

Define your own time principles—and practice saying no. Two practical techniques help:  
1. **Price your time**: Assign a concrete monetary value to an hour of your attention.  
2. **Hardcode priority tiers**: Classify incoming tasks by type (e.g., “strategic creation,” “urgent maintenance,” “optional noise”) and treat those categories as non-negotiable filters.  

In today’s world—where AI delivers instant information and passive fragmentation is the default—focus isn’t just useful. It’s rare. And therefore, *valuable*.  

Investing serious time and energy into building your focus muscle yields exceptionally high returns.  

Look forward: The future *can* be better than the past—if you steer attention toward what truly matters. So ask yourself: *What are the things that matter most—to me?*  

Countdown tools add gravity to life management. Imagine an app quietly reminding you: *You have fewer than 20 more in-person visits left with your parents.* Would that shift how you show up *this time*?  

The same applies to partners, children, friends. Many shared moments—unnoticed—are, in fact, your last.  

Guard your autonomy over time.  
Use money to *buy back* time—not just trade time for money. And when you *do* exchange time for money, make sure you genuinely enjoy the process.  

Keep life aspirational—but lower the bar on material goals. Set growth targets that fit *your* pace and values—and savor the journey, not just the milestone.  

## Four Money-Near Capabilities  

Leverage AI to build competence in four high-ROI domains: **AI-powered media**, **AI-powered sales**, **AI-powered tools**, and **AI-powered courses**.  

**Why these four?**  
1. **Traffic**: Use AI to batch-generate and manage content across dozens or hundreds of social media accounts—solving low-cost, high-efficiency audience acquisition.  
2. **Conversion**: Deploy AI to handle mid-to-lower-funnel sales (e.g., chat-based lead nurturing, follow-up sequences)—turning traffic into revenue with minimal human overhead.  
3. **Retention**: Build or re-engineer tools that directly solve painful, underserved user scenarios—using AI to slash development cost *and* deepen stickiness.  
4. **Products**: Generate highly personalized, production-ready courses in days—not months—by combining AI scripting, voice cloning, and automated video assembly.  

From here, two high-leverage actions emerge:  
- **AI + IP**: Launch “everyday person” account matrices to amplify a single creator’s reach—or scale an existing IP across niches and platforms.  
- **AI + Courses**: Spot emerging trends, lock in a course topic within 24 hours, then generate full scripts, visuals, and launch pages—all within one week.  

Core logic? Scale + automation = lower marginal cost per unit of output → higher leverage, faster iteration, real competitive advantage.  

## Account Moats  

Imagine owning 1,000 stable, consistently publishing social media accounts across platforms. That volume *itself* becomes a defensible moat—even if every account is run by an “unknown” person.  

At scale, 1,000 coordinated, quality accounts can match—or exceed—the output *and* influence of a single mega-influencer.  

Historically, this model was prohibitively expensive: content creation + account management + cross-platform scheduling added up fast.  

Now? A single operator + an integrated stack (AI content engine + RPA automation + unified dashboard) makes it feasible.  

But scale without standards breeds noise. To avoid becoming a “content landfill,” two conditions must hold:  
1. **Consistent quality**: Fine-tune AI models on domain-specific data so outputs meet a clear bar—high signal, low fluff, zero spam. If AI can’t reliably hit that bar, stop scaling.  
2. **RPA operations**: Automate publishing, comment triage, analytics pulls, and basic engagement—freeing humans to focus on strategy, creative direction, and relationship-building.  

## Cost Efficiency, Not Just Cost Cutting  

“Cost efficiency” should be baked into your operating DNA—not treated as a quarterly initiative.  

*Cost reduction* ≠ slashing budgets. It means refusing to spend on anything that doesn’t serve your core mission.  

*Efficiency gain* ≠ moving faster for speed’s sake. It means asking: *Does this acceleration create real, measurable value—or just busywork?*  

True efficiency is **value efficiency**: output per unit of meaningful input (time, capital, attention).  

A friend recently shared how his team had devolved: the only person who got yelled at daily was the only one *doing actual work*. Others stayed quiet—and unscolded—so they gradually stopped working altogether. That silent, idle headcount? Pure, unproductive cost.  

Another example: office rent. If utilization is low and vacancy high, even “modest” rent is wasteful—especially when repeated across years. In my own startup journey, we’ve burned *several million RMB* on underused office space alone. A brutal, expensive lesson.  

By contrast, investing in AI automation *is* value-efficient—it compounds: one script replaces 100 hours of manual labor, *and* improves accuracy, repeatability, and scalability.  

## Why Learn AI Programming?  

This afternoon, an AI coding instructor visited our office. He’s deeply immersed in teaching the craft—but he’s stuck on one question: *What’s the real point of learning AI programming?*  

That question struck me.  

Right now, many learners jump in for hype or anxiety relief—not clarity. And while AI coding *looks* easy, his teaching experience shows otherwise: most struggle to ship even moderately complex projects quickly.  

This confirms what we’ve seen elsewhere: AI won’t replace programmers—but it *will* massively accelerate those who know how to wield it well.  

For non-programmers, the productivity leap remains narrow—for now. Consider a tiny barrier: registering for VolcEngine, then retrieving a Doubao API token. That single step stalls *hundreds*.  

So the real question isn’t *“Should everyone learn AI coding?”*  
It’s: *What’s the realistic capability boundary—for different people?*  

- For absolute beginners: The boundary is narrow. Don’t aim for full-stack apps. Focus on *single-point wins*: auto-generating a social post, drafting a client email, designing a landing page headline. Even that level of agency transforms daily work.  
- For experienced engineers: The boundary expands dramatically—custom agents, multi-step workflows, domain-specific fine-tuning.  

The key is matching tools to *real human contexts*. Our upcoming courses will reflect this: each one targets a specific user profile, their recurring pain points, and their achievable next step—not abstract theory.  

## A Rational Decision Framework  

Most poor decisions stem not from ignorance—but from skipping rationality. I’ve made plenty: acting on gut, emotion, or urgency—then realizing, in hindsight, that pausing for *one minute* of structured thought would’ve prevented the mistake.  

A common trap? Being hyper-rational about trivial choices (“Which brand of coffee pod?”) while making life-altering ones (“Should I quit my job and start a company?”) purely on instinct. That imbalance keeps people trapped in cycles of scarcity.  

Research shows a simple intervention works surprisingly well: *Before deciding, write down 2–3 explicit reasons or criteria—even if they’re imperfect.* That tiny act pulls the brain out of autopilot and into evaluation mode.  

To reduce decision errors, try this:  
1. **Slow down big decisions**: High-stakes choices (e.g., buying property, hiring a co-founder, relocating offices) demand cognitive bandwidth. Rushing them guarantees regret.  
2. **Build and use a decision tool**: Design a lightweight framework—and make using it *mandatory* before any major call.
