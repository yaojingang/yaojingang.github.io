## How to Understand Business  

Over coffee with a friend, we distilled our shared views on what it means to truly understand business:

1. Know *how* profit is created: the cost structure, revenue streams, and return timeline.  
2. Consider your partners’ risks and rewards—not just your own. Sustainable collaboration requires mutual benefit.  
3. If a business operates legally and profitably, it *is* creating social value—even if some socially valuable work doesn’t turn a profit. These two truths coexist.  
4. People choose to work with you because you offer something distinct: quality, access, insight—or at least one of these. You must have *something* others lack.  
5. Every viable business has a simple, transparent logic. If the model feels overly complex, it likely is flawed.  
6. Mastering profit-sharing and incentive design isn’t optional—it’s central to building lasting business relationships.

## How to Avoid “Scarcity Mindset”  

(We prefer “scarcity mindset” over “poor mindset”—it names the cognitive pattern, not the person.)

1. **Reduce decisions**: Cut low-stakes choices. Each decision drains willpower—reserve it for deep learning and strategic thinking.  
2. **Stay goal-oriented**: Set clear goals *and* build feedback loops. Adjust targets and tactics based on real-world signals—not assumptions.  
3. **Ignore sunk costs**: Once a mistake is made, stop re-litigating it. Redirect energy toward next steps.  
4. **Price your time**: Assign a realistic hourly value to your time. That number instantly filters out trivial distractions.  
5. **Practice focused intensity**: You can juggle multiple goals—but at any given time, go all-in on *one*. Break through there first.  
6. **Think longer-term**: Zoom out—3 years, 5 years. Perspective reshapes urgency and reveals leverage points.  
7. **Prioritize collaboration**: Partner to fill your gaps. Long-term partnerships endure only when *all sides* walk away satisfied—so learn to distribute value, not just claim it.  
8. **Learn relentlessly**: Study people who’ve mastered your domain. Read timeless books—not just trending ones.

## What Makes a Livestream Host Succeed?  

**The deepest trait: fire in the chest, light in the eyes.**  

When interviewing hosts, watch their reaction upon hearing about the role—or after understanding its demands. Do they lean in? Does their voice lift? Does their face brighten?  

If the job fails to ignite them *in the interview*, it won’t spark viewers *on air*.  

**PSD Talent Model**  
- **P**oor upbringing (not poverty as hardship—but resource-scarce environments that cultivate grit and hustle)  
- **S**mart (not genius-level IQ, but sharp pattern recognition, quick learning, and adaptability)  
- **D**eep desire to get rich (a visceral, non-abstract drive—not greed, but agency through financial independence)  

Candidates meeting all three tend to thrive. One missing trait significantly raises failure risk—especially if they were raised affluent, show little intellectual curiosity, or feel indifferent toward wealth creation.

## Why Mechanisms Matter More Than Fixes  

Every operational problem—big or small—can be solved *systemically*, not just symptomatically.  

Example: A new hire underperforms. You hesitate to let them go—you sense potential, so you coach, extend timelines, hope for turnaround.  

That instinct is human. But the *pattern*—repeated across hires—is toxic. It quietly normalizes mediocrity and accelerates team decay.  

The real issue isn’t the employee. It’s your **hiring mechanism** (did screening miss red flags?) and **onboarding mechanism** (was training too vague or too slow?).  

True resolution means stepping back from the person—and redesigning the system.

## Focus Is Protected, Not Trained  

Maria Montessori put it plainly: *“Never interrupt a child at work—unless invited.”*  

Children are born with intense focus—especially on what captivates them. Their attention erodes not from lack of training, but from *chronic interruption*.  

A child drawing quietly. An adult walks over: *“What are you making?”* Or, *“Did you drink water yet?”*—well-meaning, but disruptive.  

If the child doesn’t answer immediately, the adult repeats—then presses—then grows impatient. That moment shatters concentration. Repeated, it trains the brain to expect fragmentation.  

Focus isn’t built like muscle. It’s *guarded* like flame. When someone is immersed, silence is your most generous act.

## How to Be Truly “Other-Centered”: Give Them *Both*  

Human nature rarely chooses between “this *or* that.” We want “this *and* that”—especially when stakes are high.  

So “being other-centered” doesn’t mean self-sacrifice. It means designing offers that satisfy *both* core human drivers: **tangible gain** (*li*, “profit”) and **social recognition** (*ming*, “name”).  

That’s *mingli shuangshou*—“fame and fortune, both received.”  

Recently, I helped a friend design a collaboration proposal. We structured it to deliver clear value *and* public visibility—crediting them meaningfully in launch materials, case studies, and talks.  

Result? They accepted swiftly—not just because the numbers worked, but because the *name* part resonated deeply. The deal flowed.

## Practical Learning Methods for Lifelong Growth  

To learn continuously, you need tools—not just motivation.

1. **Deliberate Practice**: Define one precise skill. Break it into micro-steps. Repeat—with feedback—until mastery clicks.  
2. **Feynman Technique**: Explain a concept aloud, in plain language, as if teaching a curious beginner. Gaps in clarity reveal gaps in understanding.  
3. **Trigger-Based Learning**: When a sentence, conversation, or idea *strikes you*, pause. Ask: *Why did this land? What assumption did it challenge? What hidden intuition surfaced?*  
4. **Pomodoro Method**: Work 25 minutes, rest 5. Your brain isn’t a machine—it’s a biological system that recharges in cycles.  
5. **Connection Learning**: After learning something new, ask: *Where else does this apply? How does it link to what I already know?* Build bridges—not silos.  
6. **Keyword Mapping**: For any new field, collect its 50–100 essential terms through reading and expert interviews. Map their relationships—then master the map.  
7. **Mind-Mapping**: Visually organize ideas by logic—not hierarchy. Let branches reflect how your mind actually connects concepts.
