## What Makes a Great Team?

A friend who’s played in bands for over 20 years shared his take on great teams:

* He’s always worked in tight 3–4-person units. When the people click, the output far exceeds the sum of individual abilities.  
* Working solo isn’t just prone to interruption—it often breeds conservatism.  
* The “freelance” model—jumping between collaborators or projects—is common, but tends toward mediocrity. After years of that, many lose sight of what they truly want to do.  
* In contrast, stable bands develop a *collective identity*. Members stay fixed; over time, they fuse into something new—a unit with its own voice, rhythm, and style.

My takeaway: Sustained co-creation builds rare, low-friction *mòqì*—a Chinese term for unspoken, intuitive alignment. With minimal internal friction, space opens up for bold ideas and genuine innovation.

I heard a similar point while listening to an audiobook: The era of the lone genius is over. Einstein could work alone—but over the past two decades, papers co-authored by teams consistently outperform solo work. Today’s most impactful patents are almost exclusively team-born. You don’t just need *a* team—you need *the right* team.

The key? Assemble not just *any* group, but a cohort of world-class individuals—each a top-tier expert in their domain, and each complementary to the others.

That’s where real team leverage lives. Its benefits include:

* A stable, elite team delivers “1 + 1 > 3” outcomes—not just additive, but multiplicative.  
* Long-term alignment dramatically boosts both execution speed *and* creative output.  
* Consistent collaboration helps crystallize a distinctive style and durable competitive edge.

I recently met another new friend who applies this same filter when choosing co-founders: he seeks only the absolute best practitioners in their fields. “It’s not about headcount,” he says. “It’s about finding the strongest, most complementary experts—and making it *interesting*.”

## On o3

On the final day of OpenAI’s December 2024 developer event, they unveiled o3—their latest flagship model.

Here’s how it stacks up:

* In Codeforces—a global competitive programming platform—o3 ranked **175th out of 168,076 participants**, beating **99.9%** of human coders. Its score: 2727.  
* On SWE-Bench—a benchmark for real-world software engineering tasks—o3 achieves **71.7% pass rate**, up sharply from o1-preview’s 41.3%. That means o3 correctly solves ~70% of realistic coding tasks *end-to-end*, including passing unit tests. Only ~30% now require human intervention—and even there, o3 accelerates human developers significantly.  
* On AIME 2024 (a top U.S. math olympiad exam), o3 scored **96.7%**—missing just one problem.  
* On GPQA Diamond (a PhD-level science reasoning test), o3 outperforms o1 by **10 percentage points**—and o1 already matches the average human PhD candidate.  
* On ARC-AGI (a rigorous abstract reasoning benchmark), fine-tuned o3 hits **87.5%**, surpassing the human average (**85%**).

In coding, math, logic, and scientific reasoning, o3’s cognitive performance now *exceeds* that of most humans.

For non-technical founders or general professionals, o3’s arrival matters deeply—not as a replacement, but as a *high-fidelity thinking partner*. It can surface insights, clarify assumptions, and untangle logic-heavy problems at near-expert level.

And while you no longer need to write code to ship software, AI still doesn’t replace *engineering judgment*: architecture trade-offs, user empathy, long-term maintainability, and contextual design remain deeply human. That’s where our value lies.

What’s harder—and more urgent—is cultivating the *habit* of reaching for AI *first* when tackling real-world problems. Building that reflex is the real bottleneck.

## Learning from the Best

I watched an interview with several Claude engineers on prompt engineering—and was immediately struck by its clarity and depth. I rewatched it twice, then sent it to a friend. We agreed: it’s the single best practical resource on the topic we’ve seen.

What made it exceptional wasn’t jargon or novelty—it was *precision*. They cut straight to the core mechanics, stripping away noise to reveal first principles.

That’s a hallmark of top-tier thinkers: they compress complexity into essence.

For learning, the most critical choice isn’t *what* to study—it’s *who* to learn from.

If your inputs are mediocre, your outputs will be too. So treat sourcing like strategy: go straight to the source.

To enter a new field, follow this sequence:

* Identify the field’s most influential practitioners.  
* Find their original talks, essays, or code—not summaries or secondhand takes.  
* Study not just *what* they say, but *how* they think: their framing, assumptions, and mental models.  
* Digest systematically—not passively, but by reconstructing, questioning, and connecting.  
* Integrate into your own knowledge architecture—so it becomes usable, not just stored.

Three key filters for high-signal learning:

1. **Source-first thinking**: Prioritize direct access to pioneers—not interpreters.  
2. **Essence over elaboration**: The best content distills complexity into actionable insight—not just more detail.  
3. **Quality > quantity**: One hour with a world-class mind beats ten hours of diluted content.  
4. **Active synthesis**: Like my friend did—he translated, summarized, and structured the interview into a polished doc: [Deep Dive: AI Prompt Engineering](https://eamtute8hsd.feishu.cn/wiki/C9jDwyYMoivt35kKBJuck9j3n1g)

## The Path-Dependence Principle

Over drinks with two friends, one asked: “Given our current plan, is there actually a *better* option?”

I paused—and replied: The “best” option isn’t some distant ideal. It’s the *intersection* of four conditions:  
✅ What you’re genuinely good at  
✅ What you truly enjoy  
✅ What the market needs *now*  
✅ What has credible growth potential  

That overlap is your highest-leverage starting point.

More broadly, the optimal strategy for any project or pivot is roughly **70% path dependence + 30% intentional novelty**.  
Too little continuity—pure reinvention—and you risk heroic failure. Too much inertia—zero novelty—and you stagnate.

This mirrors effective learning: peak retention happens when ~70% of new material builds on known foundations, and ~30% stretches you.

Same logic applies in the AI era: *Every existing workflow deserves an AI-native rewrite.*  
The legacy process is your 70%. AI’s capabilities—the new reasoning layer, automation, or augmentation—are your 30%.  
In some domains, that ratio flips: 50/50, even 70/30. That’s how you build authentic AI fluency.

## Learning from Brilliant Young Minds

I hosted dinner for two AI “rock stars”: my co-founder and a newly met Tsinghua graduate.

He’d interned at OpenAI, co-founded an AI startup, and reached out after reading my Feishu docs. Turns out he and my co-founder already knew each other well—so we turned coffee into dinner.

What stands out about bright young people isn’t just raw intellect—it’s *how* they channel it:

* He reads 4–5 AI research papers *every week*.  
* His grasp of complex tooling (e.g., inference optimization, eval frameworks) is unusually deep.  
* He speaks about building with infectious energy—and maintains disciplined, daily learning habits.

His WeChat intro floored me: *“Building a company that disrupts ByteDance.”*  
In today’s cautious climate, that kind of ambition is rare—and refreshing.

Even better: the tool he’s building solves a problem I’ve been hunting for months. After seeing his demo, I insisted: “Ship me the API *now*. I’m testing it tomorrow.”

We’re back in an era where audacity and execution aren’t mutually exclusive—where big ideas meet sharp execution.

## Principles *Work*

Sound principles *do* shape growth—and resilience.

Yes, “I’ve heard all the advice but still struggle” is common. But the real gap isn’t hearing—it’s *internalizing*. A principle only works once it reshapes your cognition.

There’s empirical proof: In one study, two groups of students took the same exam. One group received a 10-minute briefing on test-taking psychology beforehand. Their average score? **70 points higher**.

Why? Because stress isn’t just emotion—it’s biology. Our genes wire us to respond to threat with anxiety and cortisol: a survival mechanism honed over millennia to face predators or famine.

But modern stressors—deadlines, uncertainty, feedback—don’t require fight-or-flight. So we can *retrain* our response.

Each challenge is a chance—not just to solve a problem, but to upgrade your operating system. Reframe pressure as practice. That’s where real growth begins.
